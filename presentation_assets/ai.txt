Abstract
The continuous growth of e-commerce platforms has significantly transformed the fashion retail industry, allowing customers to browse and purchase clothing products from anywhere at any time. Despite these advantages, one of the major challenges facing online fashion shopping is the inability of customers to physically try on garments before making purchasing decisions. This limitation often leads to uncertainty regarding garment appearance, fitting, and overall satisfaction, resulting in increased product return rates and additional operational costs for retailers. Consequently, the development of intelligent Virtual Fitting Room systems has become an important research area within the fields of computer vision, artificial intelligence, and digital fashion technologies.
This project presents the design and implementation of a Virtual Fitting Room system capable of generating realistic virtual try-on results using a user image and a target garment image. The proposed system aims to provide users with a visual representation of how a selected garment may appear on their body without requiring physical interaction with the product. By leveraging recent advancements in deep learning and image synthesis techniques, the system enables realistic garment transfer while preserving the user's body structure, identity characteristics, and overall visual consistency.
The developed solution follows a multi-stage processing pipeline that begins with image acquisition and preprocessing. User images and garment images are prepared and validated before being processed by the virtual try-on engine. The system then performs garment alignment, appearance preservation, and image synthesis to generate a final output image that realistically combines the user's appearance with the selected clothing item. Special attention is given to maintaining garment details, body proportions, and natural visual transitions between different image regions to improve realism and user experience.
To ensure practical usability, the system was deployed through a web-based architecture that allows users to interact with the application using a simple and intuitive interface. The backend infrastructure was implemented using modern web technologies and integrated with the image generation pipeline to provide automated processing and result delivery. Various test cases were conducted using different clothing categories and user images to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed solution.
The experimental results demonstrate the capability of the system to generate visually coherent and realistic virtual try-on images across a variety of scenarios. The generated outputs successfully preserve garment characteristics and user identity while maintaining acceptable image quality and consistency. These findings confirm the effectiveness of the proposed approach and highlight its potential for enhancing online shopping experiences. Furthermore, the project establishes a strong foundation for future extensions, including real-time virtual try-on, mobile deployment, augmented reality integration, and personalized fashion recommendation systems.
Keywords: Virtual Fitting Room, Virtual Try-On, Computer Vision, Deep Learning, Image Synthesis, Diffusion Models, Fashion Technology, E-Commerce.

Chapter 2
Literature Review / Related Work
Summary of Existing Research and Technologies
Virtual fitting room technology has become one of the most active research areas in the intersection of computer vision, deep learning, and fashion technology. The primary objective of virtual try-on systems is to generate realistic images of users wearing selected garments without requiring physical fitting. This capability has gained significant attention due to its potential to improve customer experience and reduce product return rates in e-commerce platforms.
Early virtual try-on approaches relied on traditional image processing and geometric transformations to overlay garments onto user images. Although these methods provided basic visualization capabilities, they often failed to preserve garment details and produced unrealistic results under different poses and body shapes.
Recent advances in deep learning have significantly improved virtual fitting technologies. Several research efforts have introduced image-based virtual try-on frameworks capable of understanding both human appearance and garment characteristics. Among the most influential works in this field is VITON, which introduced a learning-based approach for transferring garments from one image to another while maintaining visual consistency. Building upon this work, VITON-HD improved image resolution and garment preservation, while HR-VITON further enhanced garment alignment and visual realism.
More recently, diffusion-based image generation techniques have demonstrated remarkable performance in image synthesis tasks. These approaches are capable of generating highly realistic images while preserving important visual attributes such as garment texture, color, shape, and user identity. As a result, diffusion-based virtual try-on systems have become a promising direction for developing more accurate and visually convincing virtual fitting solutions.
In addition to academic research, several commercial applications have adopted virtual fitting technologies. Platforms such as Zeekit and Amazon provide customers with virtual garment visualization features that enhance the online shopping experience. Other platforms focus on size recommendation and fit prediction to support purchasing decisions.
Gaps in Current Solutions
Despite the significant progress achieved in virtual try-on research, several challenges remain. Many existing solutions struggle to preserve fine garment details when transferring clothing onto different body shapes and poses. Some approaches generate visual artifacts around garment boundaries or fail to maintain realistic body proportions in the generated results.
In addition, certain virtual fitting systems require extensive computational resources, making deployment difficult in real-world applications. Other solutions depend on complex preprocessing pipelines or are limited to specific garment categories. These limitations reduce the flexibility and scalability of current systems.
Another challenge involves maintaining a balance between garment preservation and user appearance preservation. While some approaches successfully transfer garment characteristics, they may unintentionally alter facial features, body structure, or image quality, reducing the realism of the generated output.
Summary and Contribution of This Project
This project contributes to the field of virtual fitting technology by developing a Virtual Fitting Room system capable of generating realistic virtual try-on images using a user image and a garment image as inputs. The proposed solution focuses on producing visually convincing results while preserving garment appearance, user identity, and overall image consistency.
The system follows an image-based virtual try-on approach that allows users to visualize garments before making purchasing decisions. By leveraging modern deep learning and image synthesis techniques, the proposed solution aims to improve the online shopping experience and provide a practical demonstration of intelligent fashion technology applications.
Furthermore, the project establishes a scalable foundation that can be extended in future work to support real-time virtual try-on, mobile deployment, augmented reality integration, and additional fashion-related services. This contribution highlights the potential of artificial intelligence in addressing real-world challenges within the fashion e-commerce industry.

Unlike traditional online shopping platforms that rely solely on product images and size charts, the proposed solution enables users to upload their own image along with an image of a desired garment. Chapter 4
Implementation
4.1 Technologies, Tools, and Programming Languages Used
The implementation of the proposed Virtual Fitting Room system was carried out using a combination of modern programming languages, frameworks, and development tools. Python was selected as the primary programming language due to its extensive support for artificial intelligence, machine learning, and image processing applications.
PyTorch was used to support deep learning operations and image generation tasks. OpenCV and PIL were utilized for image loading, preprocessing, resizing, and format conversion. NumPy was employed for numerical computations and image data manipulation.
FastAPI was used to develop the backend API responsible for receiving user requests, processing uploaded images, and returning the generated virtual try-on results. Gradio was utilized during development and testing to provide an interactive interface for evaluating system performance and generated outputs.
The development and execution environment was based on Google Colab, which provided access to GPU resources required for image generation tasks. Ngrok was used to expose the backend application through a public URL for testing and integration purposes.
The main technologies used in this project include Python, PyTorch, OpenCV, PIL, NumPy, FastAPI, Gradio, Google Colab, and Ngrok.
4.2 Key Components and Modules of the System
The proposed system consists of several interconnected modules that work together to generate realistic virtual try-on results.
The Input Module is responsible for receiving the user image and garment image through the system interface. These images are then passed to the Image Preprocessing Module, where validation, resizing, and preparation operations are performed before processing.
The core component of the system is the Virtual Try-On Engine, which analyzes both input images and generates a new image representing the user wearing the selected garment. This component focuses on preserving garment details while maintaining user appearance and body structure.
The Backend API Module manages communication between the user interface and the processing engine. It receives image uploads, executes the generation pipeline, and returns the final output image. Finally, the Output Module displays the generated result and allows the user to view or download the generated image.
This modular architecture improves maintainability, scalability, and future system development.
4.3 Challenges Faced and How They Were Resolved
Several challenges were encountered during the implementation phase of the project. One of the main challenges was the computational resources required for image generation. Since image synthesis tasks require significant processing power, GPU resources available through Google Colab were utilized to improve performance and reduce processing time.
Another challenge involved software dependency management and library compatibility. Different packages required specific versions to function correctly together. This issue was resolved by carefully selecting and testing compatible library versions before system deployment.
Image quality and input consistency also affected the generated results. Variations in image resolution, lighting conditions, and garment appearance sometimes influenced output quality. To address this issue, preprocessing operations were applied to standardize inputs before generation.
The integration between the backend application and the image generation pipeline presented another challenge. FastAPI was used to create a structured API that efficiently handled image uploads and result delivery. Additionally, Ngrok was used to expose the application through a public URL, enabling external access and testing.
Despite these challenges, the final system was successfully implemented and was able to generate realistic virtual try-on images while maintaining satisfactory visual quality and overall system stability.

The obtained results highlight the effectiveness of modern image synthesis techniques in virtual try-on applications. The system demonstrated the ability to combine information from both user and garment images while maintaining visual coherence in the generated output.
One of the most important observations during testing was the system's ability to preserve garment appearance while simultaneously maintaining user identity. The system also requires considerable computational resources during image generation. Compared to traditional image processing techniques, virtual try-on generation involves more complex computations, which may increase processing time and limit real-time performance in some environments.
